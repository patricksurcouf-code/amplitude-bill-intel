Challenger opener
Something we hear consistently from fintech teams right now is that their users' biggest frustration isn't the core functionality of the product — it's what happens the moment something goes wrong. When a payment is delayed or a status looks off, users want an immediate answer, and the second they can't get one, trust in the entire platform starts to erode regardless of how well everything else works.

We've also seen a specific pattern where users know a payment is "processing" but have no idea whether it will arrive in two hours or two weeks — and that opacity, more than the delay itself, is what drives churn and negative reviews. The product team is often the last to know because the signal is buried in support tickets and one-star reviews rather than visible in the product data.

I'd love to understand how you're thinking about both of those problems today, because in our experience the gap between what users expect in those moments and what they actually experience is almost always larger than what shows up in internal dashboards.

What BILL's users are saying
I spent some time going through BILL's user reviews across G2, the App Store, and Trustpilot and two patterns show up in almost every complaint thread: users have no reliable visibility into payment status once a transaction is in flight, and when something goes wrong, reaching a human who can actually resolve it takes days rather than minutes.

What struck me is that both of those problems are instrumentation gaps more than they are support staffing gaps — the product team likely doesn't have a clear view of where users are stalling, what they're trying to find, or which moments are triggering the calls that overwhelm the support queue.

I have a point of view on how companies in your space have closed that gap and what the measurement looked like in the first 90 days. Would it be worth 25 minutes to walk through it?
